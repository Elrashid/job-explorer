# **Comprehensive Analysis of Literature on AI-Driven Job Market Demands in the MENA Region: A Systematic Review Framework**

The global labor market is currently navigating an era of profound structural realignment. This transformation is being catalyzed simultaneously by persistent macroeconomic fluctuations, the lingering systemic shocks of global health crises, and the unprecedented velocity of technological deployment.1 Within this global theater, the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region—particularly the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states—presents a unique laboratory of economic transition. States such as the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates (UAE) are aggressively pursuing economic diversification models aimed at decoupling their fiscal stability from hydrocarbon dependency, thereby necessitating massive state-led investments in digital infrastructure and human capital development.3

However, managing such an accelerated transition requires highly granular, real-time intelligence regarding labor market demands, emerging skill adjacencies, and the friction coefficients of occupational mobility. Historically, labor economists and policymakers derived this intelligence from lagged, survey-based occupational taxonomies that struggled to keep pace with the digitalization of the economy. Today, the proliferation of digital professional networks, most notably LinkedIn, combined with the computational processing power of Large Language Models (LLMs), has enabled the real-time extraction and synthesis of labor market data at scale.

This document provides an exhaustive, multi-disciplinary examination of the literature surrounding AI-driven job market analysis, the specific socio-economic and demographic dynamics of the MENA workforce, and the rigorous synthesis frameworks required to conduct systematic literature reviews in computational and social science domains.

## **The Theoretical Foundation of Occupational Taxonomies**

The foundational infrastructure of all labor market analysis relies fundamentally on how occupations, tasks, and human skills are classified, measured, and related to one another in an economic system. Over the past two decades, there has been a necessary epistemological shift from static, top-down governmental taxonomies to dynamic, data-driven ontologies powered by machine learning.

### **The Legacy and Latency of O\*NET**

For several decades, the gold standard for occupational classification has been the Occupational Information Network (O*NET), developed and maintained by the United States Department of Labor. Designed as a conceptual and technological upgrade to the mid-century Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT), O*NET established a highly structured content model encompassing hundreds of specific descriptors of work and workers.5 These descriptors are meticulously categorized into distinct domains, including required skills, foundational knowledge, work context, and physical or environmental exposures.6

A critical and comprehensive evaluation of the ONET system by the National Research Council highlighted its profound and pervasive utility in standardizing occupational data across federal and state agencies, aiding in career counseling, and establishing baseline health and safety exposures for epidemiological research.6 The structural rigidity of ONET allows researchers to map long-term macroeconomic trends to specific occupational cohorts with high reliability.8

Despite its rigorous theoretical grounding, O*NET suffers from inherent, structural limitations in an era characterized by rapid technological disruption. The primary vulnerability of O*NET is its latency. The taxonomy relies on extensive, prolonged survey processes and expert analyst ratings to update job profiles. Consequently, rapidly emerging skills—such as "prompt engineering," "cloud-native orchestration," or "generative AI integration"—often take years to be formally codified into the database.7 Furthermore, O\*NET operates on a relatively coarse occupational level, currently tracking approximately 1,016 distinct occupational titles organized into 23 major groups.9 This level of granularity fails to capture the subtle, continuous hybridization of roles occurring in modern knowledge economies. For example, the variance of skill requirements *within* a single broad job title (such as "Data Scientist" or "Product Manager") can frequently be more significant than the variance *between* entirely different job titles in traditional industries.

### **The Paradigm Shift to Data-Driven and Open Taxonomies**

To resolve the latency and granularity deficits inherent in static classification systems, modern labor economists and computational data scientists have turned to the massive volume of digital exhaust generated by online job boards and professional networking platforms. The methodologies pioneered by proprietary organizations such as Burning Glass Technologies (now operating as Lightcast) have demonstrated the empirical viability of extracting specific skill requirements directly from millions of online job vacancies in near real-time.

The application of this granular data extends far beyond simple recruitment analytics into the realm of profound macroeconomic observation. For instance, researchers utilizing near-universe data of online US job vacancies collected by Burning Glass have calculated labor market concentration utilizing the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) at the commuting-zone level.10 By analyzing this vast dataset, economists found that the average labor market possesses an HHI of 4,378, indicating severe concentration equivalent to merely 2.3 recruiting employers per market.10 The revelation that 60% of labor markets are highly concentrated fundamentally alters the academic understanding of wage suppression and labor monopsonies, providing occupation-level assessments that traditional administrative data simply cannot supply.11

Expanding upon these proprietary models, fully open and data-driven taxonomies have been conceptualized and deployed using unsupervised machine learning techniques. The United Kingdom's Economic Statistics Centre of Excellence (ESCoE) and Nesta collaboratively developed a robust methodology that utilizes document clustering and advanced word embeddings to extract and map skills from raw job adverts.12 By training vector embedding models on the massive corpus of job descriptions, these researchers captured the semantic, contextual relationships between skills, representing them as an interconnected network rather than a flat list.

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |

This network was then hierarchically grouped using consensus clustering and mathematical bootstrapping to ensure the stability of the resulting skill clusters.14 Unlike traditional standard occupational classification (SOC) codes, this data-driven approach yields a four-layer taxonomy where the first three layers cluster jobs based entirely on their underlying skill capability requirements, while the fourth layer distinguishes them based on offered salary indicators.12 This structural inversion allows for automated, highly personalized career recommendations and a much more fluid understanding of occupational mobility.

## **Advancements in Information Extraction from Unstructured Labor Data**

The efficacy of any data-driven labor market analysis is intrinsically linked to the accuracy and robustness of its underlying Information Extraction (IE) pipelines. Online job postings and digital resumes are fundamentally unstructured text, laden with domain-specific jargon, implicit requirements, idiosyncratic formatting, and marketing language. The evolution of IE within this specific domain directly mirrors the broader historical advancements in Natural Language Processing (NLP).

### **Traditional NLP, Keyword Matching, and Entity Normalization**

Initial computational approaches to parsing job postings relied heavily on rigid regular expressions (Regex), static keyword matching dictionaries, and rudimentary Named Entity Recognition (NER) models.15 While these methods were computationally inexpensive and highly scalable, they achieved distinctly limited success due to a profound lack of contextual and semantic understanding. A basic keyword search for the term "Python," for example, cannot disambiguate whether the employer strictly requires the skill, considers it an optional bonus, offers internal training in it, or is merely stating their company's legacy tech stack without requiring it from the applicant.

To address these shortcomings, researchers began developing comprehensive, multi-stage data pipelines. A standard architectural approach involves the automated collection of raw data via web scraping frameworks such as Scrapy, followed by storage in relational databases like MySQL.17 The critical second phase involves rigorous data refinement: normalization, deduplication, and the management of missing values.17 Entity normalization is particularly vital; it standardizes disparate textual representations (e.g., "JS," "Node.js," "Javascript") into a common machine-readable format, reducing variability and enabling accurate cross-platform analysis.18

Deduplication remains a notoriously complex challenge, as identical job roles are frequently cross-posted across multiple platforms with varying text to meet different formatting constraints or target different audiences.19 Advanced deployed solutions have proven that combining simple overlap-based character similarity with deep textual embeddings and curated, weighted lookup lists for specific technical skills leads to a significant performance boost in identifying distinct job vacancies accurately.19

### **The Integration of Transformer Models**

The introduction of transformer-based language models fundamentally altered the landscape of entity extraction in the human resources domain. Models fine-tuned specifically for labor market texts treat skill extraction as a complex token-classification problem. Research utilizing models such as JobBERT demonstrated that pretraining on vast quantities of untagged job posting data significantly improved extraction results compared to generic NLP models.20 Another highly effective pretraining methodology, ESCOXLM-R, showed that utilizing a multilingual dataset—specifically the European Skills, Competences, Qualifications, and Occupations (ESCO) classification system—combined with a robust multilingual model, further enhanced the precision of named entity recognition across varied linguistic contexts.20

The most advanced proprietary systems, such as the Job2Skills extraction architecture historically deployed on platforms like LinkedIn, incorporate both semantic salience and broader market awareness.21 These systems are designed not merely to extract existing skills, but to mathematically anticipate the rising popularity of specific capabilities and the subsequent growth of specialized job segments even before such shifts are formally reported in macroeconomic news or government statistics.21

## **Large Language Models in Structured Extraction and Evaluation**

The recent advent and widespread democratization of autoregressive Large Language Models (LLMs)—including architectures such as GPT-4, LLaMA, and Claude—has catalyzed a second paradigm shift in structured data extraction. Unlike traditional NER models or early transformers that require extensive, highly specific, manually annotated datasets for supervised fine-tuning, contemporary LLMs excel at zero-shot and few-shot extraction tasks relying purely on advanced prompt engineering.22

### **Engineering Structured Outputs**

LLMs possess the unprecedented capability to ingest wildly formatted, unstructured text—such as complex, multi-page job descriptions or deeply idiosyncratic digital resumes—and natively output highly structured data schemas, typically in JSON format.22 In production environments, this capability is frequently facilitated by robust validation libraries, such as Pydantic in the Python ecosystem, which strictly enforce the desired output schema.24 This ensures that downstream data pipelines and relational databases receive predictable, strongly typed variables despite the chaotic nature of the input text.24

The capability of these models extends to highly sophisticated contextual reasoning, allowing them to differentiate between required skills, desired traits, and general company descriptions with near-human accuracy. In specialized tasks such as Personal Information Extraction (PIE), comprehensive benchmarking studies have demonstrated that foundational LLMs significantly outperform traditional keyword and entity detection methods in accurately isolating names, educational backgrounds, and occupational histories from highly variable public web profiles.15 The proficiency of LLMs in these tasks is so profound that it has raised significant security concerns, as malicious actors can easily leverage publicly accessible LLM APIs to conduct large-scale, highly accurate data harvesting for spear-phishing campaigns, necessitating the development of novel defensive countermeasures such as prompt injection defenses.15

### **Hallucination Mitigation and Specialized Fine-Tuning**

Despite their immense power, the deployment of generic foundational LLMs for rigorous, academic data extraction introduces the acute risk of hallucination—instances where the model confidently generates non-existent skills, falsely attributes capabilities, or severely misinterprets the temporal context of a resume. To systematically mitigate this risk without losing generative flexibility, researchers have heavily explored Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), to create highly specialized, domain-locked models.

The "Skill-LLM" framework represents a premier example of this approach. By meticulously fine-tuning a lightweight language model specifically constrained for skill extraction from highly technical documents, researchers achieved precision and recall metrics that consistently outperformed both generic foundational LLMs and existing state-of-the-art specialized models like JobBERT.20 This approach proves that for highly technical, jargon-heavy documents, massive parameter counts are less critical than high-quality, domain-specific fine-tuning data.20

### **The Epistemological Challenges of LLM Evaluation**

The introduction of generative AI into data extraction poses unique, almost philosophical challenges to established evaluation methodologies. Traditional NLP evaluation metrics (such as exact string matching, Precision, Recall, or standard F1 scores) are frequently inadequate for generative models.25 These metrics struggle to gauge semantic consistency between generated outputs and rigid ground-truth labels; a model might extract "machine learning implementation" while the rigid benchmark demands "ML development," resulting in a false negative despite perfect semantic alignment.

Furthermore, when human-led data extraction is utilized as the reference standard, validation studies become highly susceptible to benchmark bias and data contamination.26 Empirical research indicates a startling reality: up to 63% of studies manually extracted by human annotators contain at least one significant data extraction error.26 Evaluating an advanced LLM against a flawed human baseline inherently suppresses the perceived performance of the model.

| Evaluation Challenge | Description | Traditional Metric Flaw | Proposed Solution / Innovation |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Semantic Rigidity** | Models generate correct answers using varying terminology. | Exact Match (EM) penalizes valid synonymous extractions. | Use of Natural Language Inference (NLI) models to verify semantic equivalence. |
| **Benchmark Bias** | Human-annotated "gold standards" contain significant error rates (up to 63%). | F1 Score assumes the benchmark is flawlessly accurate. | Iterative consensus building; double-blind human-LLM evaluation comparisons. |
| **Incomplete Schemas** | Evaluator schemas miss implicit information present in the text. | Recall assumes all possible correct answers are predefined. | **SQC-SCORE**: Fine-tuned LLMs identifying correct but omitted answers. |

To address the limitations of rigid benchmarking, novel evaluation frameworks such as SQC-SCORE (Subjective Question Correction) have been theorized and tested. SQC-SCORE actively utilizes a distinctly fine-tuned LLM, operating in tandem with a Natural Language Inference (NLI) model, to dynamically refine the matching process between the primary model's outputs and the golden labels.25 This framework explicitly addresses benchmark incompleteness by acknowledging correct, yet syntactically varied, outputs that traditional metrics would incorrectly penalize, resulting in evaluation scores that align far more closely with actual human preference and operational utility.25

## **Data-Driven Career Path Modeling and Skill Adjacencies**

The transition from fixed occupational taxonomies to dynamic, skill-based network models has profound implications for how career trajectories are modeled and predicted. Traditional career trajectory frameworks relied almost entirely on linear, hierarchical progression within deeply siloed industrial sectors (e.g., Junior Analyst to Senior Analyst to Director). However, the contemporary labor market is defined by lateral occupational mobility driven by underlying skill adjacencies.

Recent advancements in deep learning have enabled the highly accurate modeling of these complex, non-linear career transitions using sequence-based neural architectures. A prominent, peer-reviewed example is the application of Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) networks combined tightly with Self-Attention mechanisms (LSTM-ATT) applied directly to massive datasets of LinkedIn career histories.27 By programmatically scraping user profiles encompassing chronological career history, accumulated skills, formal education, and peer recommendations, researchers reframed career progression mathematically as a sequential recommendation problem.27

The LSTM-ATT model is designed to process massive multi-class classifications across thousands of distinct companies, job titles, and specific skills.27 The integration of self-attention layers is critical; it allows the neural network to dynamically weigh the significance of distant past roles against recent experience. For instance, a foundational technical role held a decade ago may hold disproportionate predictive power for a transition into a modern technical management position, a nuance that simpler Markov models miss entirely.27

In rigorous experimental setups comparing the LSTM-ATT against Multi-channel Convolutional Neural Networks (M-CNN), the sequence-based attention model consistently exhibited superior predictive performance for multi-class classification across all metrics, including Mean Percentile Rank (MPR) and Precision at 100\.27 Interestingly, the researchers conducted ablation studies by injecting external "added variables"—specifically, predicted salary estimations (derived from Payscale) and the density of social network recommendations. The empirical findings revealed a surprising dynamic: adding salary and social network data did not yield any statistically major increase in the model's predictive accuracy.27 This suggests a profound labor market reality: the intrinsic chronological sequence of acquired skills and job titles holds the overwhelming majority of predictive power regarding an individual's career mobility, superseding external financial or social network indicators.

## **Macroeconomic Shifts and the AI-Augmented Labor Market**

To understand the localized impact of these technologies on specific regions, they must first be contextualized within the broader global macroeconomic environment. The World Economic Forum's (WEF) Future of Jobs reports serve as critical barometers for global workforce demand.

The 2023 edition of the WEF report, synthesizing data from 803 global companies employing over 11.3 million workers, painted a picture of a divergent post-pandemic labor market.28 It highlighted that while high-income countries faced historically tight labor markets, lower-income regions struggled with persistent unemployment, and global real wages declined rapidly due to a severe, ongoing cost-ofliving crisis.28 Crucially, over 85% of the organizations surveyed identified the increased adoption of new and frontier technologies, alongside the broadening of digital access, as the primary trends most likely to drive business transformation over the subsequent half-decade.28

By the publication of the 2025 Future of Jobs Report, which expanded its scope to capture insights from over 1,000 employers representing 14 million workers across 55 economies, the macroeconomic pressures had evolved but remained severe.2 The increasing cost of living persisted as the top economic trend, with half of all employers expecting it to fundamentally transform their business models by 2030, despite anticipated reductions in global inflation rates.1 Furthermore, climate-change mitigation emerged as the third-most transformative trend overall, signaling a massive structural pivot toward green skills and sustainable operational frameworks.1

### **Quantitative Evidence of AI Augmentation via the Economic Graph**

The narrative surrounding Artificial Intelligence and labor demand has frequently been dominated by fears of widespread displacement. However, deep quantitative analysis leveraging proprietary datasets, such as the LinkedIn Economic Graph, presents a far more nuanced, augmentative reality. The LinkedIn Economic Graph Research Institute utilizes the platform's vast, real-time data repository to analyze macroeconomic trends, skill gaps, and workforce participation at an unprecedented scale.31

A seminal 2024 working paper analyzing the impact of GitHub Copilot—a Generative AI (GAI) powered coding assistant—provided early, rigorous evidence of AI's effect on software engineering (SWE) labor market outcomes.32 Contrary to the hypothesis that productivity-enhancing AI would reduce the aggregate demand for engineers, the analysis of LinkedIn and GitHub corporate license data revealed that companies adopting this augmentation tool actually accelerated their hiring. Specifically, high-concentration GitHub Copilot adoption led to a 3.2 percentage point higher probability of hiring new SWEs each month.32

Crucially, this AI-driven hiring surge was characterized by a distinct qualitative shift in the types of skills demanded. The adoption of the AI coding assistant led to a 13.3% increase in the demand for *non-programming skills* among new software engineering hires, alongside an increase in the non-programming skills demonstrated by existing engineers.32 As the Generative AI tool commoditized routine coding tasks, the labor market premium immediately shifted toward architectural design, project management, and cross-functional communication skills.

Further Economic Graph research highlights the critical importance of internal mobility and skills-based hiring in adapting to these technological shifts. Companies focusing on skills over formal degrees or rigid job titles were shown to grow their AI talent pipelines globally by a factor of 8.2.33 Additionally, analyses of broader demographics using this methodology revealed persistent structural challenges, such as profound gender gaps in STEM fields, where among sub-baccalaureate workers with STEM degrees, 40.6% of men remained employed in STEM compared to only 19.0% of women—a gap of 21.6% that requires targeted policy interventions to resolve.35

## **The MENA Region: A Crucible of Workforce Transformation**

Applying these advanced AI-driven analytical tools and macroeconomic frameworks to the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region reveals a labor market undergoing an exceptionally aggressive, policy-driven structural transformation. The region is characterized by a unique, highly volatile demographic dividend—a young, digitally native population that stands in stark juxtaposition to historically high youth unemployment rates, which at 25%, represent the highest regional average globally.4

### **Saudi Arabia: Vision 2030 and the Friction of Transition**

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia serves as the primary, high-stakes epicenter of this regional transformation. Historically reliant on a rentier economic model driven by oil exports—which accounted for 84% of exports and 42% of GDP—the Kingdom faced a critical juncture during the 2016 oil price decline, realizing that waiting for cyclical price recoveries was no longer a viable strategy for a population with 4.5 million young citizens poised to enter the workforce by 2030\.37

Driven by the imperative to diversify, the Vision 2030 agenda mandated a massive, rapid expansion of the private sector and the intense nationalization of the workforce. Labor market analyses conducted in 2026, comparing current outcomes against a 2015 pre-Vision 2030 baseline, indicate the staggering, sheer scale of this demographic shift: over 2.48 million Saudi nationals have successfully entered the private sector since 2020\.3 This transition has been heavily subsidized and meticulously directed by state mechanisms, notably the Nitaqat localization program and the Human Resources Development Fund (HRDF). The financial commitment is immense; in the first quarter of 2025 alone, the HRDF invested 1.83 billion SAR directly into private sector training, empowerment, and guidance, supporting 143,000 citizens.38 Consequently, the overarching labor force participation rate reached 68.2%, with Saudi national participation climbing to 51.3%.38

However, the rapid mandate for private sector employment has exposed severe, deeply entrenched structural frictions within the labor matching ecosystem. A comprehensive 2025 analysis mapping the "learn-to-earn" ecosystem identified an estimated 62 billion SAR annual loss to the Saudi economy explicitly due to career transition inefficiencies and skills mismatches among Saudi nationals (rising to 196 billion SAR when including non-Saudi workers).40 The transition from traditional educational institutions to private sector employment remains severely prolonged, with university graduates averaging nearly 40 weeks to secure their initial roles.40

Furthermore, the vulnerability to algorithmic disruption is high. Automation trends account for roughly half of total economic transition losses, placing 23% of Saudi jobs at high risk.40 Workers displaced by these trends face extended, damaging periods of unemployment, averaging 11.3 months before workforce reentry, with 40% remaining out of work for over a year.40 These metrics highlight a profound gap between the legacy capabilities produced by traditional higher education and the real-time, dynamic demands of the modernizing private sector. Implementing real-time, data-driven interventions—such as using LLMs to continuously parse job market demand and update national training curricula instantly—is mathematically essential to reducing this friction and recapturing the 62 billion SAR annual loss.40

### **The United Arab Emirates and the Rise of the "Hybrid Professional"**

In the United Arab Emirates, the intersection of rapid digital transformation and labor demand has created a distinct, highly measurable phenomenon. The UAE boasts some of the highest exposure rates to Artificial Intelligence globally, with up to 33% of its occupational base classified as highly exposed to AI-driven change.4 This exposure is aggressively met on the supply side; digital education platforms reported an 861% year-over-year surge in GenAI course enrollments across the MENA region leading into 2024\.41

A highly granular, task-level analysis of the UAE labor market—synthesizing 23,739 online job postings from major portals to construct a proprietary "Job Automatability Index"—revealed sharp, distinct occupational polarization.42 Clerical support workers face an existential substitution risk, exhibiting a mean automatability score of 53.8%, peaking at 73.0% for specific general clerk roles.42 Interestingly, the analysis found that AI exposure is statistically invariant to geography; automatability scores were nearly identical between roles located in Dubai (29.57) and Abu Dhabi (27.29), confirming that AI's impact is a structural, task-based force rather than a geographically contingent one.42 In stark contrast to clerical roles, manual labor and elementary occupations remain largely insulated due to their reliance on physical dexterity within unpredictable environments.

The most nuanced and actionable finding, however, pertains to the professional and managerial classes that dominate the UAE's knowledge economy. While the absolute demand for AI skills is surging at an unprecedented rate, the actual capabilities requested by UAE employers in job postings heavily, and somewhat paradoxically, skew toward human competencies over technical ones. In 2024, 59% of the skills cited in UAE job postings were human skills (e.g., complex communication, team management, accountability, client interaction) compared to only 41% technical skills.42

This empirical data defines the rapid rise of the "Hybrid Professional." As Generative AI models commoditize rote cognitive tasks, basic code generation, and standard data synthesis, the financial premium in the labor market shifts dramatically toward the skills that AI cannot yet replicate: high-level strategic thinking, complex human empathy, and nuanced problem-solving. Employers are actively seeking individuals who possess a baseline, functional AI literacy but who ultimately excel in the uniquely human aspects of business strategy. Consequently, the primary mechanism of AI impact within the UAE's professional class is not workforce substitution, but rather the fundamental redefinition and intense augmentation of core daily tasks.42

## **Approaches to Systematic Literature Reviews in Computational Sciences**

To rigorously synthesize the intersection of artificial intelligence, natural language processing, and regional labor economics, researchers must strictly adhere to formal Systematic Literature Review (SLR) protocols. While SLRs originated as a methodology for evidence-based clinical medicine, they have evolved into the definitive gold standard for unbiased, reproducible evidence synthesis across all empirical disciplines, including engineering and computer science.44

### **The PRISMA 2020 Statement**

The dominant, globally recognized framework for conducting SLRs is the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA). The recently updated PRISMA 2020 statement provides an exhaustive 27-item checklist and revised flow diagrams specifically designed to ensure absolute transparency, completeness, and methodological replicability in literature synthesis.45

Although the PRISMA guidelines were primarily conceptualized for the evaluation of health interventions, the core epistemological principles of PRISMA 2020 are universally applicable. The mandates for highly transparent search strategies across multiple databases, rigorously defined eligibility criteria, and the meticulous, step-by-step documentation of the study selection process (including the reporting of automation tools) translate directly to the social sciences, information systems, and computer science domains.45

### **Guidelines for Software Engineering and Information Systems**

Within the specific domain of computational science, the PRISMA framework is frequently hybridized with the pioneering guidelines proposed by Kitchenham and Charters (2007), which were specifically tailored to address the unique anomalies of Software Engineering research.46 The Kitchenham model adapts the rigid medical SLR process to accommodate the realities of computing literature. It specifically addresses issues such as the heavy prevalence and validity of peer-reviewed conference proceedings over traditional journal articles, the general lack of standardized randomized controlled trials in algorithmic testing, and the rapid, almost immediate obsolescence of technology-specific methodologies.49

Modern academic applications in Information Systems (IS) have successfully utilized these hybridized PRISMA guidelines to systematically analyze complex organizational technologies. For example, PRISMA has been utilized to map the critical success factors of Enterprise Resource Planning and Integrated Information Systems within higher education infrastructures.50 Similarly, PRISMA has been effectively deployed to systematically review the literature on data-driven future skills identification, yielding deep, structured insights into the specific analytical methodologies utilized by global researchers.21

### **Automating the Systematic Review Process with LLMs**

A frontier area of methodological research involves turning the power of artificial intelligence back upon the academic research methodology itself. The sheer, exponential volume of published literature across scientific databases makes manual, human-led SLRs increasingly laborious, time-prohibitive, and highly prone to subjective screening fatigue. Researchers are now actively designing and deploying LLMs and multi-agent AI systems to fully automate discrete stages of the PRISMA workflow, from initial metadata extraction and abstract screening to full-text ingestion and complex bias assessment.51

To ensure rigorous scientific transparency when utilizing generative AI for literature reviews, methodological extensions such as the PRISMA-DFLLM (Data-FineTuned LLM) checklist have been proposed by the academic community. This specialized framework adds specific, highly technical reporting items (Items 16 through 31\) to the traditional PRISMA checklist.51 It mandates the explicit documentation of the exact finetuning dataset used to train the reviewing LLM, the specific parameter-efficient finetuning strategy employed (e.g., QLoRA), baseline perplexity evaluations, and the precise prompt engineering constraints applied during the extraction phase.51 Empirical benchmarks of these PRISMA-DFLLM frameworks demonstrate incredibly robust alignment with human annotators, achieving up to 84% exact match rates with expert human PRISMA item-level labels across domains including Computer Science and Engineering.51

## **Strategic Synthesis and Third-Order Macroeconomic Implications**

Synthesizing the theoretical taxonomic frameworks, the evolution of computational NLP methodologies, and the highly specific regional data of the MENA market yields several critical, third-order analytical deductions that fundamentally redefine our understanding of modern labor economics.

First, the transition from deterministic, survey-based taxonomies (like O\*NET) to LLM-driven organic ontologies (via parsing LinkedIn and broader job board data) reveals an inherent, structural paradox in modern workforce planning. Generative LLMs possess the semantic fluidity to detect and extract emerging, niche skills instantly from chaotic text.20 However, if left unchecked, this extreme fluidity creates a phenomenon of "taxonomy hallucination," where distinct core skills are constantly fragmented into new, transient corporate jargon without representing any fundamental shift in actual worker capability. Therefore, the future of robust labor market analysis does not reside solely in generative extraction. Rather, the optimal analytical architecture involves utilizing LLMs to map massive flows of unstructured data directly against rigorously maintained, community-consensus vector spaces and ontologies.18 This ensures agility without sacrificing longitudinal measurement capability.

Second, the structural analyses of the MENA region—particularly the profound divergence observed in Saudi Arabia between high macro-level job creation (2.48 million new private sector roles) 3 and massive micro-level friction (the 62 billion SAR annual loss due to skills mismatch) 40—suggests that state-led economic diversification is significantly outpacing the adaptive capacity of traditional human capital supply chains. The 40-week average lag for university graduates entering the workforce represents a catastrophic information asymmetry failure within the economy.40 Platforms capable of parsing real-time LinkedIn data using sequence-based neural architectures (such as the LSTM-ATT career modeling approach, which relies on the intrinsic sequence of skills rather than external variables) 27 are not mere corporate recruitment tools. They represent critical, national economic infrastructure required to reduce the friction coefficient of the labor matching function. By probabilistically modeling skill adjacencies, MENA governments can transition away from broad, slow-moving educational mandates and instead deploy surgical, micro-targeted reskilling subsidies precisely where the algorithmic demand is clustering.

Finally, the empirical findings regarding Generative AI's specific impact on knowledge workers, particularly within the highly exposed UAE market 42, subvert the traditional, alarmist narrative of technological displacement. The evidence clearly indicates that as Artificial Intelligence effectively commoditizes technical execution, routine coding, and basic data synthesis, it does not destroy the role of the knowledge worker. Instead, it enforces a rapid, mandatory pivot toward the "Hybrid Professional." The empirical reality that 59% of demanded skills in highly AI-exposed Gulf markets are distinctly human-centric—coupled with LinkedIn's finding that AI coding assistants increase demand for non-programming skills by 13.3% 32—implies a profound shift in labor valuation. The ultimate operational bottleneck in the future digital economy is not pure technical capacity, which is increasingly abundant and algorithmic. Rather, the bottleneck is strategic alignment, cross-functional empathy, complex managerial synthesis, and accountability.

In summation, leveraging Large Language Models to conduct automated, PRISMA-compliant systematic reviews of unstructured job posting data offers an unprecedented, real-time mechanism to monitor labor market evolution. For the rapidly transforming MENA region, harnessing these advanced computational extraction tools is not merely an academic exercise in natural language processing; it is a vital, urgent policy instrument mathematically necessary to navigate the complexities of post-oil economic diversification and mitigate the friction of the artificial intelligence era.
